01

Introduction to Data Analytics & Business Intelligence

Modern organisations run on data. Every sale, click, message and sensor reading is a record of something that happened — and when you study enough of those records, patterns emerge that help leaders make better choices.

Data analytics examines data to discover useful information and support decisions. Business Intelligence (BI) is the set of tools, dashboards and reports that make those insights visible across an organisation. Together they answer one question: "What is our data telling us, and what should we do about it?"

Lesson goal: By the end of Lesson 1 you'll be able to explain what data is, where it comes from, why it matters, and how the data analysis lifecycle moves an organisation from a question to a decision.

05

Data as a business asset — "the new oil"

Just as oil powered the industrial revolution, data now powers the information age. But like crude oil, raw data has little value until it's refined — collected, cleaned, analysed and interpreted into insight.

Data science and analytics exist to transform raw data into valuable insights. The goal is never "more data" — it's better decisions.

10

Data strategy

A data strategy is a long-term plan covering the technology, processes, people and rules needed to manage an organisation's information assets — aligning data work with business goals.

  • Unlocks operational efficiency, faster decisions and new revenue streams.
  • Guides architecture decisions — databases, storage formats and integrations.
  • Breaks down data silos, reduces duplication, ensures quality and integrity.
  • Builds a data-driven culture while maintaining security, privacy and compliance.
11

Why everyone needs to be data literate

Data literacy is the ability to read, understand, question and communicate with data. It's no longer just for analysts — marketers, managers and operations staff all make better decisions when they can interpret a chart or challenge a number.

Being data literate doesn't mean being a statistician. It means knowing what a number represents, where it came from, and whether you can trust it before acting on it.

13

Hypothesis formulation & testing in business analytics

A hypothesis is a testable statement about your data. Hypothesis testing gives analysts a disciplined way to check whether a pattern is real or just chance — before making a costly decision.

Null hypothesis (H₀)

The "no effect / no difference" assumption — e.g. "the new layout did not change conversion."

Alternative hypothesis (H₁)

The claim you want to test — e.g. "the new layout increased conversion."

A simple workflow: State H₀ & H₁ → choose a test → set significance (0.05) → analyse → compare p-value → decide

Activities & Reflection

Check your understanding

Use these prompts for class discussion, self-study or your learning journal. There are no single "right" answers — they're designed to make you think like an analyst.

Reflection questions
  • List three sources of data in your daily life. What could a business learn from each?
  • Explain "data is the new oil" in your own words. Why must data be refined before it's valuable?
  • Pick a business you know. Which decisions could be improved with better analytics?
  • How would you explain the difference between Data Analytics, Data Science and BI to a friend?
Discussion prompts
  • Why is data literacy now considered a skill for everyone, not just analysts?
  • Walk through the data lifecycle for a food-delivery app: where is data generated, and how does it flow to a decision?
  • What risks come from acting on poor-quality or unethically collected data?
Mini activity — frame a question
  • Choose a simple business scenario (e.g. a small café).
  • Apply the Ask → Prepare → Process → Analyse → Share → Act framework: one sentence per step.
  • Form one testable hypothesis (H₀ and H₁) the café could investigate with data.
